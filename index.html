John D. Carmack

John Carmack programming.

This page is about John Carmack, a computer programmer from Kansas. He has revolutionized the computer gaming industry by creating software that pushed the limits of hardware.

1970 | Born August 20, 1970

1984 | Carmack broke into a school to steal an Apple II computer. Carmack spent the next year in juvenile detention.

1988 |Enrolled at the University of Missouri. He took only computer classes, and after two semesters dropped out to devote himself full time to programming.

1990 | Landed a steady programming job with Softdisk in Shreveport, Louisiana. One of his first jobs was developing a two-dimensional video arcade game called Invasion of the Vorticons.

1991 | Founded id software with John Romero, Adrian Carmack and Tom Hall

1992 | Released Wolfenstein 3-D. This game introduced the First Person Shooter genre as well as Share-ware. Share-ware allowed users to sample the software before purchasing.

1993 | Released Doom. This computer game featured ultra realistic graphics and a compelling experience. It became one of the best selling video games of all time. The U.S. military has used Doom to train soldiers for combat.

1994 | Released Doom II

1996 | Released Quake, which had more realistic graphics and introduced multiplayer play over the internet. Architects have used the graphics technology of Quake to construct virtual buildings.

1997 | Released Quake II. Building upon the success of the former and improving all of its functions.

1999 | Released Quake III Arena. An online multiplayer game.

2004 | Released Doom III. An updated version of the original Doom with updated graphics.

2005 | Released Quake IV

2011 | Released RAGE

2013 | Steps down from id Software to become Chief Technology Officer (CTO) for Oculus VR, a virtual reality company

2019 | Left Oculus VR, to pursue research on artificial general intelligence.

"His contributions to real-time computer graphics allowed for the creation of more complex games with rich details on early personal computers that lacked video cards and operated on MS-DOS. He is also credited as a catalyst for the explosive growth and evolution of the medium of gaming.
